Instrument endorsements often fail for the same reason other endorsement records fail: the wording is broader than the training record behind it. The cleaner standard is to write only what the actual training supports.

## Start with the scenario

Before touching wording, identify the exact scenario:

- Instrument knowledge test authorization
- Instrument practical test readiness
- Instrument proficiency check context
- Additional instrument-related recurrent training

Each of those has a different recordkeeping purpose. One generic sentence should not be stretched across all of them.

## Keep the endorsement tied to the record

The endorsement should be easy to trace back to:

- The training completed
- The instructor who evaluated it
- The rating or certificate context involved

That means scope matters. If the underlying training record is narrow, the endorsement should be narrow too.

## Common drafting mistakes

- Copying a sentence from an old logbook without checking the current scenario
- Treating instrument and practical test language as interchangeable
- Writing a broader authorization than the training notes support
- Leaving the wording technically complete but operationally unclear

## The better workflow

Draft after the prerequisites are verified, not before. That keeps the endorsement aligned with the real training path instead of forcing the record to match a template.

## Practical takeaway

Good instrument endorsement language is boring in the best way. It is narrow, explicit, and easy to justify. If the wording looks polished but the scope is fuzzy, it still needs work.
